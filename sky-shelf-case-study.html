- Overview -

There are moments when you feel frustrated while browsing through your cloud storage trying to locate the folder where you stored that one assignment you need the next day or that one photo you would like to show your family and friends. Users come across various challenges and restrictions with cloud storage services such unintuitive designs, limited storage and messy interfaces.

Sky Shelf is the solution to your problems. Sky Shelf aims to create a simple interface where users can see all of their content on their dashboard. In other words, all of the users’ content is now centralized. This eliminates all the extra clicking and having to navigate to half a dozen pages to see all of their content.

- Discovery -

Competitive Analysis

I started my research by analyzing the competition because one of the biggest questions left unanswered by the client’s brief was who/what the target market was going to be. I chose dropbox, box and google drive because those seem to be the heavy hitters that have a larger user base.  In analyzing the competition, It gave me an understanding of the market. I studied their strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats through a SWOT analysis. Through this I was able to identify what they were doing correctly, and leverage that, as well as analyze their growth areas, so that I could address those problems as they pertained to my users.

Dropbox has their strength with it’s simple design, however, it offers a small amount of free storage which causes users to switch to another service. Box on the other hand offers a great amount of free space but its weakness is that it has overly complicated features that some users struggle with. Google drive appears to be the best of both worlds since it offers a large amount of free space with a pretty intuitive design. However, Google Drive is only available to those who have a gmail, so if a user does not have a gmail, he or her has to go through of creating a gmail account. These were some of the strengths and weaknesses that helped frame my cloud storage service. See my competitive analysis here.

Survey

I followed my competitive analysis research with a survey distributed online to multiple social media platforms/forums and email contacts. I wanted to understand which features users considered most import and useful, but also, their frustrations and what they currently use for storing information online.

What do you mainly use cloud storage for?
Why influenced you to choose your current cloud storage service?

Other findings:
50% of the users use cloud storage at least 5 days a week
100% of the users use a laptop/desktop to access it while 67% of them also use their phone for access
89% of the users felt that being able to upload any type of file was the most important feature
39% of the users rarely used collaboration features

From the results I was able to figure out that users would like to see an improvement on the ease of use of cloud storage services. A lot of the services are still very bloated which make them a bit challenging to use and the users would like for it to be more straightforward.

Interview & Personas

After analyzing the results from my survey, I interviewed a couple of the survey respondents to dig deeper into their responses and clarify my understanding of the problem and my potential solution. From the conversations I had with the users, I identified two major user groups, from which I developed into these personas.

One group consisted of the student/worker who needs cloud storage to seamlessly collaborate with his or her colleagues without having to alter their schedule. The other group consisted of the user who uses cloud storage for personal use, one who saves photos and memories and then shares it with family and friends.

- Information Architecture -

User Stories

My next step in the process was to create user stories to help prioritize the important features for both a new user as well as an existing user. See the complete user stories here. (The red are high importance, the orange are in the mid range and the yellow are of low importance.)

User Flows

I converted my users stories into user flows to visualize how the user would accomplish those stories with my product. Each user flow was created with a high priority story being the starting point. The medium and low priority stories were embedded within the high priority stories.

Low Fidelity Wireframes & User Testing

I used Axure to draw out some low fidelity mockups so that I could quickly create a visual of all of the abstract ideas and concepts I had created. This helped both the client and I quickly analyze it and identify weak points in the design. I created mockups of the onboarding process as well as the dashboard and its functions.

User Testing

After creating these mockups, I exported my work to Invision, where I created a clickable prototype so that I could test my users and receive feedback on the functionality of my website. You may find a sample script here.

Feedback generated from my clickable prototype informed me that:

  • The text in the paragraphs was too small.
  • The paragraphs were too lengthy and were causing the users to skip over them.
  • The arrows and the labels on the quick tour were not very noticeable.
  • A lot of functions were missing navigation element such as back arrows.
  • Users wanted a getting started pdf in case they forgot the information from the quick tour.
  • Users wanted the option to sign up with social media.
  • Users loved the bubbles/bucket dashboard, they said they liked having everything in front of them, the less clicking the better.

- Branding & Identity -

Branding

I moved away from the design of the project's interface and onto the design of the project's branding. I wanted a brand name a user could feel comfortable using in a work environment as well as with their family and friends. Sky Shelf is meant to make the user think of a storage method (shelf) that is up in the sky since he/she can't see it.  By knowing that the storage shelf is up in the sky out of reach, it gives the user a peace of mind knowing no one can reach his/her stuff. In addition, the sky is the perfect space for storage since its vast and practically endless. I wanted Sky Shelf to evoke a feeling of peace and tranquility on the users. This is achieved through the logo, typography and color palette.

I began my brainstorming with a word-association exercise surrounding the ideas of storage and centralizing content. This lead to a very large list of compound words that went through various rounds of user testing till It came down to the winner “Sky Shelf”. Although Sky Shelf was not my top choice, I went with it because it was the name the user’s prefered and after all this design was going to be for the users. Upon the selection of my brand name I began sketching multiple iterations of a potential logo. I started off by playing with the wording, I wanted a typeface the was curvy and bubbly to go with the theme of a soft cloud.

I played around with the ideas of having a shelf literally be in the sky but I realized this had too much going on. I wanted a simple design users could easily remember. The next iterations involved simple shapes involving the sky and a shelf. I looked at these designs and thought they were pretty stupid up until those “stupid” designs suddenly caused a spark in my imagination. It had suddenly occurred to me “What if I create a shelf that was literally an element from the sky,?!”  In my case that would be a cloud because it went well with my brands soft and tranquil identity. I created a couple quick iterations of this potential logo and finally created a more detailed sketch that would represent my final logo.

Style Guide

Sky Shelf’s primary blue hue was chosen for various reasons. The first being that it would remind the user of the sky since it is blue. Second, to evoke a feeling of trust and confidence in the user so that they would feel safe uploading and storing their files on Sky Shelf. The green hue was chosen as an accent color due to its strong emotional correspondence with safety. We want the user feeling safe when pressing on the green call to action buttons such as signing up, uploading and sharing files.

- Design -

High Fidelity Wireframes & User Testing

After taking into account the feedback from my low fidelity user tests, I started to transform my mockups into high fidelity wireframes by applying color, illustrations, and the proper typography I had selected in my style guide. Upon completion of my high fidelity wireframes I had second thoughts about some of the potential styles for call to action buttons as well as the landing pages for both my mobile and web designs. The way to solve some of these seconds thoughts was by putting it in front of the users. I created a couple schemes that put my second thoughts to rest and I created preference tests on Usability Hub for my users to select which schemes they felt were most successful. The feedback I received from users was extremely helpful, click here to view the tests and results. I made the modifications needed based off the feedback and created my clickable prototypes for my users to test.

Results generated from my clickable prototype informed me that:

  • The onboarding process was successful since all of the users quickly signed up.
  • The process for adding an item could be improved by adding the option for a user to remain on the same page to upload more than one item.
  • The upload a file feature could be added to more locations throughout the app.
  • The organization feature was successful because users completed it with little to no problem.

- Reflection -

Sky Shelf was the first project where I completed both the User Experience research and the User Interface design. I gained an appreciation for doing research, surveying, testing, creating wireframes and making multiple iterations of my work in order to come out with the best outcome. One of the biggest lessons I learned after completing Sky Shelf is the importance of understanding the users through interaction. I learned to make informed design decisions that are based off of testing, researching and analyzing. Had I not interacted with the users, I would have had a hard time figuring out in which direction to go with the design. The more feedback I received from the users, the more constraints I had, the better the design became.

After creating the Case Study, I came to realize how the design process made sense. I was surprised how each small step/checkpoint helped solve the overall design problem. I compared my final design with existing cloud storage services and I was very satisfied with the fact that my design was way different yet it still accomplished all of the important tasks my user wanted. I felt this project was a success, everything from the design process worked. I would not say that something did not work, however I wish I would have done more preference testing. I received so much valuable feedback from the preference tests I did create.  If given more time I would have continued massaging the interface and I would have done more preference tests. As a designer I feel like a design is never done, I constantly look at my work and think to myself “What if I add this? What If I change this?”.
